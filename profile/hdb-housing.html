SG Public Housing Dashboard

Singapore's public housing policies are internationally acclaimed, with many attributing our economic miracle to our public housing policies. However, there is recent discussions on the affordability of our public homes. Since the public home resale data set available to public, I wanted to let data speak for itself by creating a dashboard to monitor our public housing market in my own way. Best viewed on desktop.

Instead of focusing on a single metric like median or mean prices, I wanted to see the distribution of our public resale prices across months and quarters. Boxplot charts does this well because it shows the median prices, 25th and 75th percentile, and also any outliers within the period category. Our boxplots show that the separation between the expensive and cheap public homes have increased in the past few years.

I also overlapped a bar and line chart that tracks the total number of million public dollar homes, and the percentage of million dollar public homes over the total number of public homes sold. Not only we see a rising number of million dollar public homes sold Singapore, there is also a growing trend of these million dollar homes forming a larger percentage of overall public homes sold in Singapore. However, this said, the percentage of million dollar homes is still a relatively small number.

This stacked bar chart shows the number of public home sales, grouped by price categories determined by me. We see growing number of public home sales that are in the range of SGD 500-800k, SGD 800K-1M and SGD >1M. In recent years, there are a shrinking number of public homes sold in the range of SGD 300-500K, and barely any public homes sold that are < 300K.

This chart is similar to the one above, but looking at percentages instead to make changes in proportions across different price categories more obvious. We can see that expensive public home sales are forming a larger proportion of sales in the resale market. The percentage of >1M public homes is still quite small, but the growing trend does seem a bit worrying.
